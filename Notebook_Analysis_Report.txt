2.2  Adv_EDA_and_Forecasting.ipynb (Forecasting Notebook)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Steps performed:
  1. Loaded raw CSV via kagglehub.
  2. Converted 'last_updated' to datetime.
  3. Sorted by ['location_name', 'last_updated'] to preserve temporal order
     within each city.
  4. Created working copies: dataset0, dataset, dataset1.

For the Forecasting pipeline:
  a) Dropped irrelevant / redundant columns:
       country, latitude, longitude, timezone, last_updated_epoch,
       last_updated, temperature_fahrenheit, feels_like_fahrenheit,
       feels_like_celsius, gust_mph, wind_mph, pressure_in, precip_in,
       visibility_miles, sunrise, sunset, moonrise, moonset,
       moon_phase, moon_illumination
     (temperature_celsius was extracted as target Y separately)

  b) Removed rows where the target (temperature_celsius) was null.

  c) Categorical Encoding:
       All object/category columns were label-encoded after lowercase + strip.

  d) Physically Impossible / Corrupt Value Removal:
       Columns were clipped to valid physical ranges (set to NaN if out of
       range), for example:
         humidity             : [0, 100]
         wind_kph             : [0, 410]
         pressure_mb          : [870, 1084]
         uv_index             : [0, 20]
         air_quality_PM2.5    : [0, 1000]
         air_quality_us-epa   : [1, 6]
         (and more…)

  e) Feature Engineering:
       pm_ratio       = PM2.5 / (PM10 + 1e-6)
       wind_u         = wind_kph × cos(wind_degree)
       wind_v         = wind_kph × sin(wind_degree)
       humid_pressure = humidity × pressure_mb
       wind_degree column was then dropped.

  f) Removed duplicate and constant columns.

  g) Outlier Clipping:
       Each numeric column clipped to [1st percentile, 99th percentile].

  h) Correlation Filter:
       Only features with |Pearson r| > 0.03 with the target were retained.

  i) High-Null Column Removal:
       Columns with > 50% missing values were dropped.

  j) KNN Imputation (k=5):
       Remaining missing values filled using K-Nearest Neighbours imputation.

3b. Adv_EDA_and_Forecasting.ipynb — Advanced EDA & Anomaly Detection
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ANOMALY DETECTION: Z-Score, IQR & Modified Z-Score Outliers
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Three standard outlier detection techniques were applied across numeric columns
of the Global Weather Repository (sorted by location and time):

  METHOD 1 — Z-Score Method:
    • A value is flagged as an outlier if |z| > 3 (i.e., more than 3 standard
      deviations from the column mean).
    • Applied to all numeric columns of interest.

  METHOD 2 — IQR (Interquartile Range) Method:
    • Lower bound = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR
    • Upper bound = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
    • Values outside this range are flagged as outliers.

  METHOD 3 — Modified Z-Score Method:
    • Uses the Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) instead of std deviation.
    • More robust to heavily skewed distributions.
    • Threshold: |Modified Z-Score| > 3.5.

  VISUALISATION:
    • A 1400×800 figure showing the distribution of flagged outliers across
      methods, enabling comparison of their sensitivity.
    • Results show that different methods detect different volumes of outliers,
      with the Modified Z-Score being the most conservative and the IQR method
      being the most aggressive for non-Gaussian features like PM2.5.

  KEY ANOMALY FINDINGS:
    • Air quality metrics (especially PM2.5, Ozone, CO) exhibit the most
      extreme outliers, especially in urbanised Asian regions.
    • Temperature outliers likely correspond to extreme weather events or
      data recording errors.
    • Wind speed outliers can represent tropical cyclones or data artifacts.

8c. LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) Recurrent Neural Network
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MOTIVATION:
  Temperature is inherently time-series data. LSTM networks can capture temporal
  dependencies (e.g., overnight cooling after a sunny day, inertia in weather
  patterns) that tabular models like XGBoost miss.

DATA PREPARATION FOR LSTM:
  • Sequences created per location to avoid cross-location contamination.
  • window_size = 24 (24 time steps ~ 24 hours of sensor readings per city).

SECTION 9: KEY INSIGHTS & CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

1. GEOGRAPHY DRIVES TEMPERATURE:
   Latitude is the most powerful predictor of temperature (R² contribution
   verifiable across RF, GB, permutation, and Pearson correlation). The higher
   the absolute latitude, the colder the region. This relationship is nearly
   linear and consistent globally.

2. WIND IS THE PRIMARY POLLUTION DISPERSER:
   Wind speed exhibits the strongest negative correlation with PM2.5 levels.
   Stagnant air (low wind) traps particles near the surface, worsening air
   quality dramatically. This has strong implications for urban air quality
   forecasting and policy.

3. ASIA FACES THE SEVEREST AIR QUALITY CHALLENGES:
   Multiple analyses (Top-10 PM2.5 countries, continental box plots, and
   spatial bubble maps) converge on the same conclusion — South and East Asian
   cities experience PM2.5 levels far exceeding WHO guideline thresholds
   (≥35 μg/m³ constitutes "Unhealthy" for sensitive groups).

4. TROPICAL REGIONS DOMINATE PRECIPITATION:
   South America and Oceania (driven by tropical and subtropical climates)
   show the highest average precipitation, while continental arid zones (Middle
   East, North Africa cluster in Egypt) show near-zero rainfall.

5. TEMPERATURE INERTIA CAPTURED BY LSTM:
   The LSTM's per-location, sequential approach demonstrates that temperature
   has strong temporal autocorrelation — yesterday's temperature is an excellent
   predictor of today's. Even with just 2 epochs, the model nearly matches the
   global XGBoost on R² (82% vs 86%).

6. ANOMALY DETECTION REVEALS DATA QUALITY ISSUES:
   Z-score, IQR, and modified Z-score analyses reveal that air quality columns
   (especially PM2.5, CO, Ozone) contain extreme outliers, many of which likely
   reflect genuine pollution spikes (e.g., wildfire events, industrial
   accidents) rather than sensor error. Physical range validation during
   preprocessing appropriately handles impossible readings.

7. FEATURE ENGINEERING ADDS VALUE:
   Engineered features (wind_u, wind_v decomposition; pm_ratio; humid_pressure)
   capture more nuanced physical relationships than raw columns alone, and they
   pass the correlation threshold filter, confirming their relevance.

8. GLOBAL WEATHER DATA IS RICH BUT HETEROGENEOUS:
   With 136,828+ records spanning diverse climates, geographies, and seasons,
   the dataset supports both macro-level continental comparisons and micro-level
   per-city temporal modelling. Careful preprocessing (outlier clipping, KNN
   imputation, range validation) is critical for obtaining reliable model
   outputs.
